<p>I found this strange niche of <strong>micro-poetry</strong>, or <strong>haiku</strong>, on Twitter and after writing a few of my own it soon became a bit of an obsession for me. I started a self-imposed 100 day challenge - a haiku a day for 100 days, sharing my progress on Twitter.</p><h2 id="what-is-a-haiku">What is a haiku?</h2><p>A haiku is a micro poem, which has a format of 3 lines, and also a structure of 5, 7, 5 for the number of syllables per line, though I follow that very loosely, if at all. That's all there is to it!</p><h2 id="why-haikus">Why haikus?</h2><p>I found they give just enough structure and constraint that I can’t possibly overthink or dither with indecision. I just write. And they are so short that there is no obstacle in terms of the perceived effort involved.</p><p>It's a great expressive and creative outlet that teaches you to look at things around - it’s a mini dose of mindfulness, or more correctly perhaps, <em>presentness</em>.</p><p>It enables you to discover or filter what matters. Nature. Love. Fascination. The universe. Consciousness. Whatever. This is often emergent and changes with mood which is interesting in its own right.</p><h2 id="observations">Observations</h2><p>Haiku isn’t the only form of micro poetry. There is also something called <strong>senryū</strong>. Senryū tend to be about human foibles while haiku tend to be about nature. Some of mine might be more like senryū than haiku. But, who cares. It’s my party.</p><p>Sometimes I had an urge to explain the haiku. This may be as bad form as explaining a joke. I don’t know. Sometimes I did explain the context. Other times I mentally noted the urge but left it alone to pass.</p><p>Other times I experimented with photos. And in fact I like this, especially when it’s one of my own photos. You’ll see I’ve kept photos in the collated list below.</p><p>My favourite inspiration for a haiku is a walk or a run. No headphones, no distractions, just in the present and in nature. It’s this beautiful dance where I spend the time twirling words and phrases in my mind. There is no rush. It’s unhurried perfection.</p><h2 id="tips-for-starting-out">Tips for starting out</h2><p>Don’t overthink it, just look at something, anything, and write three short lines. That’s it! Do it. If you’re on Twitter then use #haiku and see what others are writing about too - it’s a great community.</p><h2 id="my-favourites-top-10">My favourites (top 10)</h2><p>Here are ten of my favourite haikus. The power of habit is important and in some cases quality comes from quantity.</p><p>Here are my faves:</p><blockquote>Eyes wide with wonder <br>Christmas lights reflecting <br>In those perfect orbs — Day 100</blockquote><blockquote>Maiden of the water <br>Flowing over rock and root <br>Sings a siren’s song — Day 83</blockquote><blockquote>Sickle moon <br>To hang my hat and <br>Breathe in the Universe — Day 70</blockquote><blockquote>Toes under the covers, <br>Elbows touching. <br>Separately together — Day 63</blockquote><blockquote>Inquisitive mind. <br>Turning over every leaf, <br>Opening every door — Day 43</blockquote><blockquote>Parting clouds, <br>Revealing nature’s heart and wisdom, <br>That I may stare in awe — Day 38</blockquote><blockquote>Sparkly magic wand <br>Wielded by a two-year-old <br>“Turn you into a frog Daddy!” — Day 33</blockquote><blockquote>Disturbed slumber. <br>I rise, in body not spirit, <br>Wishing the day away — Day 26</blockquote><blockquote>Holding on tight <br>To a little hand.
